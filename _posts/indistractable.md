# Indistractable: How to Control Your Attention — Data Sampler Summary

## Core Insight
> Distraction isn't caused by technology—it's driven by internal discomfort; mastering attention requires managing emotions first, then engineering your environment.

---

## Representative Samples: 4

### Sample 1: The 10-Minute Rule for Urges
**Scenario**: Mid-task, you feel the pull to check your phone. Instead of resisting or giving in, you tell yourself: "Not yet—I can check in 10 minutes." You stay on task, observe the boredom or frustration with curiosity, and often the urge fades before the timer ends.
**Pattern**: *Surf the urge, don't fight it*. Delaying—not denying—reduces psychological reactance. Discomfort is the root trigger; acknowledging it weakens its power over your behavior.

### Sample 2: Timeboxing Beats To-Do Lists
**Scenario**: Instead of writing "Write report" on a to-do list, you block 9:00–10:30 AM on Tuesday for it. If you stayed focused during that window, it's traction—even if the report isn't finished. Unplanned scrolling during that block? That's distraction.
**Pattern**: *You can't call something a distraction unless you know what it's distracting you from*. Timeboxing creates intentionality; traction is defined by forethought, not activity type.

### Sample 3: The Orange Vest Signal
**Scenario**: At Kaiser Permanente, nurses wore bright orange vests while dispensing medication to signal "do not interrupt." Result: an 88% reduction in medication errors over 36 months. Eyal adapts this: headphones, a desk sign, or a status message can protect your focus blocks.
**Pattern**: *External triggers are hackable*. A simple, visible cue reduces interruptions by making your boundaries legible to others—and to yourself.

### Sample 4: "I Don't" vs. "I Can't"
**Scenario**: When tempted to scroll social media during dinner, saying "I don't check my phone at the table" (identity statement) proves far more effective than "I can't check my phone" (restriction). Research shows 80% adherence with "I don't" vs. 10% with "I can't."
**Pattern**: *Identity drives behavior*. Framing choices as expressions of who you are—not rules you follow—creates sustainable self-regulation without willpower depletion.

## Key Generalizations

| Concept | Core Truth |
|---------|-----------|
| Traction vs. Distraction | Both pull you; only intention distinguishes them. Same action, different outcome based on planning. |
| Internal Triggers Dominate | ~90% of distractions stem from uncomfortable emotions (boredom, stress), not external pings. |
| Precommitment > Willpower | Design friction *before* temptation strikes. Effort, price, and identity pacts automate good choices. |

## Formula
> **The Trigger Audit**: When distracted, ask: (1) *What am I feeling right now?* (internal trigger), (2) *Is this action pulling me toward or away from my values?* (traction check), (3) *What pact can I make now to protect my next focus block?* (precommitment).

---

## Conclusion
Becoming indistractable starts internally: name the discomfort driving the urge, then delay—not deny—the distraction. Next, timebox your values into your calendar so traction has a home. Finally, hack external triggers and lock in precommitments. Attention isn't found; it's built. Start with one 10-minute urge-surfing session today.
