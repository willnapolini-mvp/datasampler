# 100 Ways to Improve Your Writing (Gary Provost) — Data Sampler Summary

## Core Insight
> Good writing is not about following every rule — it is about making deliberate choices in sound, structure, and word selection so the reader stays engaged from the first sentence to the last.

---

## Representative Samples: 5

### Sample 1: Write Music
**Scenario**: Provost writes an entire paragraph using only five-word sentences, letting it drone on until the reader feels the monotony in their bones — then abruptly shifts to a long, rolling crescendo that demands attention. "This sentence has five words. Here are five more words. Five-word sentences are fine. But several together become monotonous... Now listen. I vary the sentence length, and I create music. Music. The writing sings."
**Pattern**: Sentence length controls reader energy. Short sentences create punch and rest; medium lengths carry narrative flow; long sentences build momentum and importance. The ear tires of repetition — give it rhythm.
**Why this matters**: Writing is not just semantic; it is acoustic. Readers hear words in their heads even when reading silently. Monotonous prose feels like a stuck record; varied prose feels alive.

### Sample 2: Use Dense, Familiar Words
**Scenario**: Instead of writing "once a month," write "monthly." Instead of "impossible to imagine," write "inconceivable." But when writing for a general audience, choose "jaw" over "mandible" — even if your dentist uses the fancier term daily. "If you are writing a story just for your dentist, use mandible. But if you are writing for everybody else, use the more familiar word, jaw."
**Pattern**: Density compresses meaning (one word doing the work of three); familiarity ensures the word lands. Never use a long word where a short one works. Never use a fancy word where a known one communicates.
**Why this matters**: Every unnecessary word is friction between the idea and the reader. Dense, familiar words respect the reader's time and cognitive bandwidth.

### Sample 3: Put Emphatic Words at the End
**Scenario**: Provost shows how the same factual sentence shifts emphasis depending on word order: "By June first please send me a check for $107.12" stresses the amount. "Please send me a check for $107.12 by June first" stresses the deadline. "On June first the check for $107.12 should be sent to me" stresses the recipient.
**Pattern**: The last words of a sentence receive the most auditory weight — readers remember what ends, not what middles. Place the information you want stressed at the sentence's end.
**Why this matters**: Sentence architecture controls attention. Writers often bury their most important point in the middle, where it dies.

### Sample 4: Steal Small, Steal Often
**Scenario**: Hear a great metaphor in the Sunday paper? Borrow it. Overhear a perfect anecdote at a barber shop? Use it. Catch a joke that illustrates your point? Adapt it. "If you steal from one writer, it's called plagiarism, but if you steal from several, it's called research."
**Pattern**: Originality does not require inventing every element from scratch. Steal small (a phrase, not a chapter), steal from many (blend sources so no single influence dominates), and transform what you take into something new through context.
**Why this matters**: Writers who wait for pure originality produce nothing. The best creators are magpies — they collect shiny fragments and assemble them into structures no one has seen before.

### Sample 5: Stop When You Get to the End
**Scenario**: Provost's editing method for endings: cross out the last sentence and ask, "What does the reader lose?" If the answer is "nothing" or "I don't know," delete it. Repeat with the next sentence. Keep crossing out until you reach a sentence you cannot do without. If that sentence sounds final and leaves the reader in the intended mood, you are done.
**Pattern**: Most writers overstay their welcome. The impulse to keep explaining, to keep justifying, to keep saying goodbye sixteen times at the door — it weakens the finish. A strong ending is earned by everything that came before it, not by everything you add after.
**Why this matters**: The last impression is the lasting impression. A good ending feels inevitable, not labored.

## Key Generalizations

| Concept | Core Truth |
|---------|-----------|
| **The Ear Is the First Editor** | If it sounds wrong when read aloud, it *is* wrong. Rhythm reveals problems that eyes miss. |
| **Every Word Must Work** | No ornamental adjectives, no filler transitions, no throat-clearing openings. Each element either carries meaning or carries the reader forward — or it goes. |
| **The Reader, Not the Writer, Decides** | Writing is not self-expression; it is communication. Dense words, emphatic endings, and rhythmic variety all exist to serve the reader's experience, not the writer's ego. |

**The Provost Formula**: *Good Writing = (Dense Words × Varied Rhythm × Structural Awareness) − Unnecessary Words*

## Conclusion
Provost's 100 tips reduce to a single discipline: **be intentional about every choice**. Vary your sentences on purpose. Select your words on purpose. End where the meaning ends. Apply this by taking any paragraph you've written, reading it aloud, and asking of every single element: *Does this serve the reader?* If the answer is no, cross it out.
