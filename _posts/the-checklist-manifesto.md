# The Checklist Manifesto — Data Sampler Summary

## Core Insight
> Failure in complex systems stems not from ignorance but from ineptitude—checklists protect expertise by ensuring known steps are applied consistently under pressure.

---

## Representative Samples: 4

### Sample 1: The WHO Surgical Checklist
**Scenario**: Gawande's team deployed a 19-item checklist across eight global hospitals with three pause points: before anesthesia, before incision, before patient exit. Deaths fell 47%, complications dropped 36%, infections halved.
**Pattern**: *Critical steps need explicit verification*. Even experts skip "obvious" steps under stress. Checklists don't teach new skills—they safeguard existing knowledge by making the invisible visible.

### Sample 2: Pronovost's Central Line Protocol
**Scenario**: At Johns Hopkins ICU, a 5-step checklist for inserting central lines: wash hands, clean skin, sterile drapes, mask/gown/gloves, sterile dressing. Within a year, line infections dropped from 11% to zero, saving $2M and preventing 8 deaths.
**Pattern**: *Targeted simplicity beats comprehensive complexity*. A short, focused checklist on high-impact "killer items" delivers disproportionate ROI. Five steps, rigorously applied, outperform hundred-page manuals.

### Sample 3: The B-17 "Flying Fortress" Crash
**Scenario**: In 1935, a Boeing B-17 prototype crashed—not from mechanical failure, but because the pilot forgot to release a new locking mechanism. The plane was too complex for memory alone. The solution: a pre-flight checklist. Aviation safety transformed overnight.
**Pattern**: *Complexity demands external memory*. When cognitive load exceeds working memory (~7±2 items), checklists offload recall, freeing experts to focus on judgment, not step-tracking.

### Sample 4: Van Halen's Brown M&Ms Clause
**Scenario**: David Lee Roth demanded a bowl of M&Ms with all brown ones removed. If promoters found a brown M&M, the band knew to line-check the entire production—the clause was a canary for attention to detail.
**Pattern**: *Verification beats assumption*. A simple, observable test reveals systemic diligence. Checklists aren't just task lists—they're diagnostic tools that expose whether the system itself is functioning.

## Key Generalizations

| Concept | Core Truth |
|---------|-----------|
| Ineptitude > Ignorance | Most failures occur not because we don't know, but because we forget to apply what we know under pressure. |
| Brevity Enables Discipline | Checklists work when short (5-9 items) and focused on "killer steps"—long lists get ignored, defeating their purpose. |
| Pause Points Create Safety | Every checklist needs a clear trigger (before incision, after onboarding) to interrupt autopilot and force verification. |

## Formula
> **Checklist Design Rule**: (Critical Steps ≤ 9) × (Clear Pause Point) ÷ (Ambiguity) = Adoption  
> *Keep it short, tie it to a moment, write it plainly.*

---

## Conclusion
Deploy checklists where complexity meets consequence: client handoffs, quality reviews, financial closes. Choose **Do-Confirm** for experienced teams (verify after execution) or **Read-Do** for high-stakes sequences (execute step-by-step). Remember: the checklist isn't the goal—consistent outcomes are. Start with one critical process, test it with your team, and iterate. Discipline compounds; a five-item list used reliably beats a perfect manual gathering dust.
