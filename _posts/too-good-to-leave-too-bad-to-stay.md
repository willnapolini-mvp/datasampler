# Too Good to Leave, Too Bad to Stay — Data Sampler Summary

## Core Insight
> Relationship ambivalence isn't solved by weighing pros and cons—it's resolved through diagnostic questions that reveal whether your relationship has a viable foundation.

---

## Representative Samples: 4

### Sample 1: The "Best of Times" Test
**Scenario**: Jennifer, successful externally but confused internally, reflected on her marriage's peak moments and realized they were never *truly* fulfilling—just "fine."
**Pattern**: You can repair what broke, but you cannot build on what never existed. If your relationship's best moments lacked genuine connection, the foundation itself is the problem.

### Sample 2: The "Permission to Leave" Question
**Scenario**: Michael, a therapist stuck in a troubled marriage, imagined a higher power granting him guilt-free permission to leave—and felt immediate relief.
**Pattern**: Relief at the thought of freedom is data, not betrayal. When external obligations (guilt, fear, duty) are removed, your emotional response reveals your true preference.

### Sample 3: The Physical Affection Indicator
**Scenario**: A couple goes months without initiating touch—not due to stress, but because the desire itself has vanished. No conflict, just absence.
**Pattern**: Sustained lack of mutual physical affection signals a deeper emotional disconnect. Sex isn't the issue; it's the barometer. When touch feels obligatory or absent, the relational circuit is broken.

### Sample 4: The "All Problems Solved" Final Filter
**Scenario**: Imagine every conflict—money, chores, communication—magically resolved today. Would you still feel uncertain about staying?
**Pattern**: If ambivalence persists even in a problem-free scenario, the issue isn't the relationship's circumstances—it's the person. This question isolates compatibility from fixable friction.

---

## Key Generalizations

| Concept | Core Truth |
|---------|-----------|
| Diagnostic > Deliberative | Yes/no questions cut through ambivalence faster than pros/cons lists, which trap you in circular reasoning. |
| Foundation > Fixability | Relationships fail not because problems exist, but because the core connection was never strong enough to withstand them. |
| Action > Intention | What you *do* (avoiding your partner, making solo plans) reveals your decision before your mind admits it. |

## Formula
> **The Relief Test**: *If imagining life without your partner brings more relief than grief, your subconscious has already decided.*

---

## Conclusion
Stop analyzing. Start diagnosing. Use targeted yes/no questions to bypass ambivalence and uncover whether your relationship has a viable core. If foundational elements—genuine past joy, mutual affection, shared values—are absent, no amount of problem-solving will create them. Clarity isn't cruel; ambivalence is.
