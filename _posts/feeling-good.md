# Feeling Good: The New Mood Therapy — Data Sampler Summary

## Core Insight
> Depression is not caused by events, but by distorted interpretations of them—and you can rewire your mood by learning to identify and challenge ten predictable cognitive errors.

---

## Representative Samples: 4

### Sample 1: The Triple-Column Technique
**Scenario**: A student thinks, "I failed one quiz, so I'm a total failure." Using Burns' method, they write the automatic thought in Column 1, identify the distortion ("all-or-nothing thinking") in Column 2, and craft a rational response in Column 3: "One quiz doesn't define my worth; I can learn and improve."
**Pattern**: *Externalize to evaluate*. Writing thoughts down creates psychological distance, allowing objective challenge. This simple structure turns abstract self-criticism into a solvable logic problem.

### Sample 2: The "Should Statement" Trap
**Scenario**: A professional tells herself, "I should never make mistakes at work." When she misses a deadline, she spirals into guilt and shame. Burns shows that "should" statements create unrealistic standards that guarantee emotional punishment.
**Pattern**: *Rigid rules breed suffering*. Replace "should" with flexible preferences ("I'd prefer to do well, but I'm human"). This reduces guilt while preserving motivation.

### Sample 3: The Pleasure-Predicting Sheet
**Scenario**: A depressed person predicts a walk will rate 2/10 for enjoyment and avoids it. After completing the activity and rating the actual experience (6/10), they see their negative forecast was wrong—building evidence against future pessimism.
**Pattern**: *Behavior precedes motivation*. Action generates data that disproves depressive predictions. You don't wait to feel better to act; you act to feel better.

### Sample 4: The Double Standard Technique
**Scenario**: Someone berates themselves: "I'm so stupid for that error." Burns asks: "Would you say that to a friend who made the same mistake?" When the answer is no, the person practices applying the same compassion to themselves.
**Pattern**: *Self-criticism is a choice, not a fact*. Treating yourself with the kindness you'd offer others disrupts the depression cycle and builds sustainable self-worth.

## Key Generalizations

| Concept | Core Truth |
|---------|-----------|
| Thoughts → Feelings | Emotions follow interpretations, not events. Change the thought, change the mood. |
| Distortions Are Systematic | Cognitive errors aren't random; they follow ten identifiable patterns you can learn to spot. |
| Action Over Insight | Understanding isn't enough. Lasting change requires behavioral experiments that test negative predictions. |

## Formula
> **The 3-Question Challenge**: When a negative thought arises, ask: (1) *Is this thought based on facts or feelings?* (2) *What's a more balanced way to see this?* (3) *What would I tell a friend in this situation?* Use the answers to rewrite the narrative.

---

## Conclusion
Burns' method works because it's trainable, not mystical. Start by catching one distortion per day—perhaps "all-or-nothing thinking"—and apply the Triple-Column Technique. Over time, you build a mental immune system against depressive spirals. The goal isn't to eliminate negative thoughts, but to stop believing them automatically. Mood follows mindset; mindset follows practice.
