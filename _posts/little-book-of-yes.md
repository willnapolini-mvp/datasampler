# The Little Book of Yes — Data Sampler Summary

## Core Insight
> Getting "Yes" isn't about charisma—it's about designing requests around predictable psychological triggers that make agreement feel natural, reciprocal, and personally relevant.

---

## Representative Samples: 4

### Sample 1: The Handwritten Post-it
**Scenario**: Psychologist Randy Garner doubled survey response rates simply by adding a short handwritten note with the recipient's name on a Post-it attached to his request.
**Pattern**: *Personalization activates reciprocity*. When effort signals genuine regard, people feel a social obligation to reciprocate. Generic requests fade; personalized ones stick.

### Sample 2: The Hotel Towel Sign
**Scenario**: Hotels testing towel-reuse messages saw a 33% jump when signs stated "Most guests *in this room* reused towels" versus generic "Most guests reuse towels."
**Pattern**: *Specific social proof beats broad norms*. People follow peers they identify with. The more locally relevant the reference group, the stronger the conformity pull.

### Sample 3: The Three-Mint Tip Strategy
**Scenario**: Restaurant servers increased tips 23% by giving one mint, starting to leave, then turning back to offer a second "just for you" mint—creating a moment of unexpected personalization.
**Pattern**: *Surprise + personalization = disproportionate impact*. Small gestures feel larger when they break routine and signal individual attention. Timing and framing amplify value.

### Sample 4: Ask Takers for Advice
**Scenario**: When dealing with colleagues who rarely reciprocate help, researchers found asking them for *advice* (not favors) increased cooperation—granting prestige shifted their mindset.
**Pattern**: *Reframe the ask to align with identity*. People resist owing, but lean into feeling expert. Advice requests disarm defensiveness and invite engagement without triggering debt-aversion.

---

## Key Generalizations

| Concept | Core Truth |
|---------|-----------|
| Reciprocity is directional | Give first, give personally, and the obligation to return flows naturally—without asking. |
| Proof is local | "People like you" persuades more than "people in general." Specificity creates relevance. |
| Framing beats force | How you position a request (advice vs. favor, scarcity vs. abundance) matters more than the request itself. |

## Formula
> **The YES Trigger**: *Personalize* the ask + *Anchor* to a relevant peer behavior + *Frame* as low-cost/high-value = Higher agreement probability.

---

## Conclusion
Stop pushing for agreement; start designing for it. Before any request, ask: "Have I given value first? Is my social proof specific? Does my framing make 'Yes' feel easy and identity-consistent?" Apply these samples as templates—not scripts—and you'll hear "Yes" more often, ethically and sustainably.
