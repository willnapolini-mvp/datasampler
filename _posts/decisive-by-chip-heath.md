# Decisive: How to Make Better Choices — Data Sampler Summary

## Core Insight
> Bad decisions stem from four predictable psychological traps—not lack of intelligence—and a simple four-step WRAP process can systematically counteract them.

---

## Representative Samples: 4

### Sample 1: The Vanishing Options Test
**Scenario**: A manager debating "Should I fire this underperforming employee or not?" applies the vanishing options test: *What if both choices disappeared?* This forces exploration of alternatives—retraining, role change, or performance coaching—revealing options hidden by binary framing.
**Pattern**: *Narrow Framing* blinds us to plural possibilities. Asking "What else could I do?" or "What would I do if my current options vanished?" widens the decision space and prevents false dilemmas.

### Sample 2: The 10/10/10 Rule
**Scenario**: An entrepreneur tempted to accept a lucrative but misaligned partnership asks: *How will I feel about this in 10 minutes? 10 months? 10 years?* The short-term excitement fades; the long-term regret sharpens. She declines, protecting her core mission.
**Pattern**: *Short-term Emotion* distorts judgment. Creating temporal distance via the 10/10/10 framework shifts focus from immediate feelings to enduring values, enabling choices aligned with your future self.

### Sample 3: "Ooching" Instead of Leaping
**Scenario**: Before launching a costly new product line, a startup runs a low-risk "ooch": a pop-up stall at a local market to test demand. The data reveals weak interest, saving months of wasted development—and pivoting the team toward a stronger opportunity.
**Pattern**: *Confirmation Bias* leads us to seek reassuring data. Small, fast experiments ("ooches") reality-test assumptions with real-world feedback, replacing speculation with evidence before major commitments.

### Sample 4: The Pre-Mortem Tripwire
**Scenario**: A nonprofit planning a major campaign sets a tripwire: *If donor sign-ups don't hit 30% of target by Week 3, we pause and reassess.* They also run a pre-mortem: *Imagine it's one year later and this failed—why?* This surfaces hidden risks and builds adaptive contingency plans.
**Pattern**: *Overconfidence* makes us underestimate uncertainty. Bookending outcomes (pre-mortem/pre-parade) and setting tripwires creates humility and agility, turning rigid plans into learning systems.

## Key Generalizations

| Concept | Core Truth |
|---------|-----------|
| Spotlight Effect | We decide based on what's immediately visible, not what's relevant. Better decisions require manually shifting the light. |
| Process > Analysis | A robust decision *process* beats perfect analysis of flawed options. WRAP structures the process to counter bias. |
| Distance Creates Clarity | Emotional and temporal distance don't eliminate feeling—they reveal which feelings matter. |

## Formula
> **WRAP = Widen Options + Reality-Test Assumptions + Attain Distance + Prepare to Be Wrong**  
> *Apply one WRAP step to every high-stakes decision. If you skip one, you're vulnerable to its corresponding villain.*

---

## Conclusion
Stop trusting gut instinct alone. When facing a meaningful choice, run the WRAP checklist: generate more options, seek disconfirming evidence, create emotional distance, and plan for surprise. Decisiveness isn't about speed—it's about installing a bias-resistant process. As the Heaths show, better decisions aren't luck; they're architecture.
