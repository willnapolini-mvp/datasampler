# PREDICTABLY IRRATIONAL — Data Sampler Summary

## Core Insight
> Human decisions aren't random mistakes—they follow systematic, predictable patterns driven by hidden psychological forces we can learn to recognize and navigate.

---

## Representative Samples: 4

### Sample 1: The Decoy Effect (Relativity Bias)
**Scenario**: MIT students chose between three Economist subscriptions: Online-only ($59), Print-only ($125), or Print+Online ($125). With the useless "decoy" option present, 84% picked the combo. Remove the decoy, and preference flipped dramatically.
**Pattern**: We don't evaluate value in isolation—we judge by comparison. Marketers exploit this by designing choice architectures where one option exists solely to make another look superior.

### Sample 2: The Zero-Price Effect
**Scenario**: In experiments, people eagerly took a "free" Hershey's Kiss even when paying 1¢ for a superior Lindt truffle was objectively better value. The word "FREE" triggered emotional arousal that overrode rational cost-benefit analysis.
**Pattern**: Free isn't just a price—it's an emotional trigger that blinds us to trade-offs. We overconsume, overcommit, or choose inferior options simply because the downside feels invisible.

### Sample 3: Social Norms vs. Market Norms
**Scenario**: A daycare charged late-pickup fines to reduce tardiness. Result? Late pickups *increased*. Parents swapped guilt (social norm) for a fee (market norm), treating lateness as a purchasable service.
**Pattern**: Two operating systems govern behavior: social norms (trust, reciprocity) and market norms (prices, contracts). Mixing them corrupts motivation—and restoring social norms after market intrusion is extremely difficult.

### Sample 4: Hot-State vs. Cold-State Decision Making
**Scenario**: When asked in a calm state, men predicted they'd stop sexual advances if a partner said "no." In an aroused state, the same men reported they'd likely continue—revealing a dramatic gap between anticipated and actual behavior.
**Pattern**: We cannot accurately predict our future choices when emotions run high. Rational planning fails unless we pre-commit guardrails *before* entering emotionally charged situations.

## Key Generalizations

| Concept | Core Truth |
|---------|-----------|
| Relativity | Value is contextual, not absolute—we judge by comparison, often to irrelevant anchors. |
| Emotional Override | Arousal (anger, desire, excitement) bypasses rational deliberation; design for your worst self. |
| Norm Collision | Social and market motivations are incompatible; introducing money into relationships changes the game permanently. |
| Predictability | Irrationality isn't chaos—it's patterned. Map the pattern, and you can design better choices. |

**Rule-of-Thumb**: *When stakes are high, assume your future self will be irrational—and build constraints now that your later self can't easily override.*

## Conclusion
Apply these samples by auditing your own decisions: What anchor is shaping your price expectations? Is "free" distorting your judgment? Are you mixing social favors with market transactions? By recognizing these predictable traps, you can redesign environments—personal, professional, or policy-level—to nudge toward better outcomes. The goal isn't perfect rationality; it's *strategic awareness* of the hidden forces shaping your choices.
