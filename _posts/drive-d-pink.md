# Drive: The Surprising Truth About What Motivates Us — Data Sampler Summary

## Core Insight
> Traditional "if-then" rewards undermine performance on creative tasks; lasting motivation flows from autonomy, mastery, and purpose—not carrots and sticks.

---

## Representative Samples: 5

### Sample 1: The Candle Problem
**Scenario**: Participants given a candle, tacks, and matches must attach the candle to a wall. When offered cash prizes for speed, those promised rewards actually solved the puzzle *slower* than the control group—because extrinsic incentives narrowed focus and blocked creative insight.
**Pattern**: For non-routine, conceptual work, *if-then rewards impair performance*. Autonomy and intrinsic curiosity outperform transactional incentives when problems require flexible thinking.

### Sample 2: Atlassian's FedEx Days
**Scenario**: Australian software company Atlassian gives employees 24 hours to work on *any* project they choose—so long as they demo results the next day. This "FedEx Day" model sparked innovations like bug-fixing tools and product features that became core offerings.
**Pattern**: *Autonomy fuels innovation*. When people control their task, time, team, and technique, they invest more deeply and produce higher-value outcomes than under micromanagement.

### Sample 3: Google's 20% Time
**Scenario**: Google engineers spend one day per week on self-directed projects. This policy birthed Gmail, Google News, and AdSense—products that generated billions in revenue, all originating from unstructured exploration, not top-down mandates.
**Pattern**: *Structured autonomy scales*. Allowing 20% freedom within organizational boundaries creates a pipeline of employee-driven innovation while maintaining strategic alignment.

### Sample 4: Rowe Furniture's Turnaround
**Scenario**: Facing decline, Rowe Furniture let assembly-line workers self-organize: choose tasks, set pace, solve problems collaboratively. Productivity rose 30–50%, defects dropped, and morale surged—without changing pay or equipment.
**Pattern**: *Mastery requires agency*. When workers control how they improve, they engage in deliberate practice and take ownership of quality—outperforming externally monitored systems.

### Sample 5: Wikipedia vs. Encarta
**Scenario**: Microsoft's paid-expert Encarta encyclopedia competed against Wikipedia's volunteer-driven model. Wikipedia won—not through financial incentives, but by tapping contributors' desire to share knowledge and serve a global mission.
**Pattern**: *Purpose amplifies effort*. When work connects to a cause larger than oneself, people contribute more persistently and creatively than extrinsic rewards can purchase.

## Key Generalizations

| Concept | Core Truth |
|---------|-----------|
| Reward Mismatch | Extrinsic motivators work for algorithmic tasks but sabotage heuristic (creative) work—design incentives to match task type. |
| Autonomy Architecture | Freedom isn't chaos; high-performing autonomy operates within clear goals and boundaries (task/time/team/technique). |
| Purpose Multiplier | People will exert extraordinary effort for meaning; connect daily work to contribution, and motivation compounds. |

## Formula
> **Motivation = Autonomy × Mastery × Purpose**  
> If any element approaches zero, intrinsic drive collapses. Maximize all three multiplicatively—not additively—for exponential engagement.

---

## Conclusion
To motivate in the modern economy, replace control with context. Give people autonomy over *how* they work, create pathways for mastery through feedback and challenge, and anchor work in purpose beyond profit. Apply the AMP framework: audit tasks for reward-sensitivity, design autonomy guardrails, and articulate the "why" behind every goal. Motivation isn't something you do *to* people—it's something you unleash *within* them.
