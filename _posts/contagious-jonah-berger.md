# Contagious: Why Things Catch On — Data Sampler Summary

## Core Insight
> Word-of-mouth drives 20–50% of purchasing decisions, and virality follows six predictable psychological patterns—not luck or influencer magic.

---

## Representative Samples: 5

### Sample 1: The $100 Cheesesteak
**Scenario**: At Barclay Prime in Philadelphia, owner Howard Wein launched a cheesesteak priced at $100 with wagyu beef, foie gras, and truffles. Locals mocked it—until foodies began ordering it precisely because the absurd price made the story worth telling.
**Pattern**: Inherent remarkability creates *Social Currency*. When sharing something makes us look like insiders or in-the-know, we talk. Exclusivity and status signaling drive transmission more than modest utility.

### Sample 2: Kit Kat + Coffee
**Scenario**: In 2007, Hershey's paired Kit Kat with coffee under the tagline "A break's best friend." By anchoring the chocolate to an everyday ritual Americans repeat multiple times daily, sales jumped 8% within months and 33% within a year—without changing the product.
**Pattern**: *Triggers* matter more than inherent interest. "Top of mind means tip of tongue." Products linked to frequent, relevant environmental cues generate sustained word-of-mouth, whereas merely interesting products fade quickly.

### Sample 3: Susan Boyle's Audition
**Scenario**: An unemployed 47-year-old woman walked onto *Britain's Got Talent* and sang "I Dreamed a Dream." The shock and awe of the performance generated over 100 million views. Berger's analysis of thousands of *New York Times* articles confirmed the same pattern across content types.
**Pattern**: *Emotion* drives sharing through physiological arousal, not positivity. High-arousal states—awe, anger, excitement, humor—prime us to share. Low-arousal emotions like sadness or contentment suppress transmission entirely.

### Sample 4: The Upside-Down Apple Logo
**Scenario**: Steve Jobs insisted the Apple logo on MacBooks face outward (appearing upside-down to the user) so observers in cafes would see it right-side-up. Similarly, iPod's white headphone cords served as "behavioral residue" advertising the product long after purchase.
**Pattern**: *Public* observability drives imitation. If people cannot see a behavior or product, they cannot imitate it. Designing for visibility—making the private public—creates social proof and organic contagion.

### Sample 5: The Vietnamese Nail Salon Diaspora
**Scenario**: In 1975, actress Tippi Hedren taught nail care to 20 Vietnamese refugees near Sacramento. The skill required no English, carried practical value, and spread through tight-knit community stories. Today, Vietnamese Americans comprise 80% of California's nail technicians.
**Pattern**: *Stories* are Trojan horses that carry ideas. When a product or message is integral to a narrative—not a detachable tagline—it spreads naturally. This case demonstrates all six principles compounding across decades.

## Key Generalizations

| Concept | Core Truth |
|---------|-----------|
| The Messenger Myth | Content virality depends on the message's design, not the status of who shares it. A good joke is funny even from an average person. |
| The Offline Majority | Only ~7% of word-of-mouth happens online. Contagious design must work at dinner tables and grocery aisles, not just feeds. |
| Arousal Over Valence | Any emotion can spark sharing if it raises physiological arousal. Calm happiness kills transmission as surely as sadness. |

## Formula
> **Rule of 100**: For products under $100, highlight percentage discounts (e.g., "25% off"). For products over $100, highlight absolute dollar savings (e.g., "$250 off"). The same savings feels bigger when the presented number looks larger.

---

## Conclusion
To engineer contagion, stop chasing viral platforms and start designing for human psychology. Use the STEPPS checklist—**Social Currency, Triggers, Emotion, Public, Practical Value, Stories**—to ensure your idea is worth talking about, easy to remember, and effortless to share. Virality is not lightning; it is architecture.
